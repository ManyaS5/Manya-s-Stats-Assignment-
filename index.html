Hypergeometric Distribution

The "No Put-Back" Rule

What is it? (Layman's Terms)

The Hypergeometric distribution is what you use when you are picking items from a group without replacing them. Because you don't put the item back, the probability changes with every pick.

Real Life Analogy

Imagine a jar with 10 cookies: 7 Chocolate Chip and 3 Oatmeal. You grab 4 cookies to eat. You obviously can't put a cookie back after you eat it! The chance of getting a Chocolate Chip cookie changes after every cookie you take out.

The Formula

$$ P(X=x) = \frac{{^K C_x} \times {^{N-K} C_{n-x}}}{^N C_n} $$

What this formula really means (in simple words):

It's a fraction. The top part counts "all the ways to get the specific winners you want" AND "all the ways to get the losers you don't want". The bottom part is just "total ways to pick any group of items".

Mnemonic to Remember

"Big N, Big K, little n, little x"

  • N (Big N): Total population size (The whole jar)
  • K (Big K): Total successes in population (All the Chocolate Chip cookies)
  • n (little n): Sample size (How many you grab)
  • x (little x): Number of successes you want (How many Chocolate Chip cookies you hope to get)

Solved Examples

Example 1: The Deck of Cards

Question: From a standard deck of 52 cards, you draw 5 cards. What is the probability of getting exactly 2 Aces?

Step 1: Identify the parameters

  • N (Total population) = 52 cards
  • K (Total successes in population) = 4 Aces
  • n (Sample size) = 5 cards drawn
  • x (Desired successes) = 2 Aces

Step 2: Apply the formula

$$ P(X=2) = \frac{{^4 C_2} \times {^{48} C_3}}{^{52} C_5} $$

Step 3: Calculate each combination

  • ⁴C₂ (Ways to pick 2 Aces from 4) = 4!/(2!×2!) = (4×3)/(2×1) = 6
  • ⁴⁸C₃ (Ways to pick 3 Non-Aces from 48) = 48!/(3!×45!) = (48×47×46)/(3×2×1) = 17,296
  • ⁵²C₅ (Total ways to pick 5 cards from 52) = 52!/(5!×47!) = 2,598,960

Step 4: Calculate the probability

P(X=2) = (6 × 17,296) / 2,598,960

P(X=2) = 103,776 / 2,598,960

P(X=2) ≈ 0.0399 or 3.99%

Answer: There is approximately a 4% chance of drawing exactly 2 Aces when you draw 5 cards from a standard deck.

Example 2: Quality Control

Question: A box contains 20 light bulbs, of which 5 are defective. If you randomly select 4 bulbs, what is the probability that exactly 1 is defective?

Step 1: Identify the parameters

  • N = 20 total bulbs
  • K = 5 defective bulbs
  • n = 4 bulbs selected
  • x = 1 defective bulb desired

Step 2: Apply the formula

$$ P(X=1) = \frac{{^5 C_1} \times {^{15} C_3}}{^{20} C_4} $$

Step 3: Calculate each combination

  • ⁵C₁ = 5!/(1!×4!) = 5
  • ¹⁵C₃ = 15!/(3!×12!) = (15×14×13)/(3×2×1) = 455
  • ²⁰C₄ = 20!/(4!×16!) = (20×19×18×17)/(4×3×2×1) = 4,845

Step 4: Calculate the probability

P(X=1) = (5 × 455) / 4,845

P(X=1) = 2,275 / 4,845

P(X=1) ≈ 0.4696 or 46.96%

Answer: There is approximately a 47% chance of getting exactly 1 defective bulb.

Poisson Distribution

The "Rare Events" Rule

What is it? (Layman's Terms)

The Poisson distribution predicts the number of times an event occurs in a fixed interval of time or space. It's used for things that happen independently and at a constant average rate.

Real Life Analogy

Think of a call center. On average, they might get 5 calls per minute. But in any specific minute, they might get 3, or 8, or 0. Poisson tells us the probability of getting exactly 8 calls when the average is 5.

The Formula

$$ P(X=x) = \frac{e^{-\lambda} \lambda^x}{x!} $$

What this formula really means (in simple words):

It calculates the chance of 'x' events happening, based on the average rate 'lambda' (λ). 'e' is just a special math number (approx 2.718).

Mnemonic to Remember

"Lambda is the Average"

  • λ (Lambda): The average number of events (The rate)
  • x: The specific number of events you are checking for
  • e: Euler's number (constant ~ 2.718)

Solved Examples

Example 1: The Call Center

Question: A call center receives an average of 3 calls per minute. What is the probability of receiving exactly 5 calls in a minute?

Step 1: Identify the parameters

  • λ (Average rate) = 3 calls per minute
  • x (Specific number) = 5 calls

Step 2: Apply the Poisson formula

$$ P(X=5) = \frac{e^{-3} \times 3^5}{5!} $$

Step 3: Calculate each component

  • e⁻³ ≈ 0.0498 (using e ≈ 2.718)
  • 3⁵ = 3×3×3×3×3 = 243
  • 5! = 5×4×3×2×1 = 120

Step 4: Calculate the probability

P(X=5) = (0.0498 × 243) / 120

P(X=5) = 12.1014 / 120

P(X=5) ≈ 0.1008 or 10.08%

Answer: There is approximately a 10% chance of receiving exactly 5 calls in a minute.

Example 2: Website Traffic

Question: A website receives an average of 2 visitors per hour. What is the probability of getting exactly 0 visitors in an hour?

Step 1: Identify the parameters

  • λ = 2 visitors per hour
  • x = 0 visitors

Step 2: Apply the formula

$$ P(X=0) = \frac{e^{-2} \times 2^0}{0!} $$

Step 3: Calculate each component

  • e⁻² ≈ 0.1353
  • 2⁰ = 1 (anything to the power of 0 is 1)
  • 0! = 1 (by definition)

Step 4: Calculate the probability

P(X=0) = (0.1353 × 1) / 1

P(X=0) ≈ 0.1353 or 13.53%

Answer: There is approximately a 13.5% chance of getting zero visitors in an hour.

Before you leave the topic, here are the five random questions from the topic:

1. Why does the Hypergeometric distribution converge to the Binomial distribution as N approaches infinity, and what is the specific condition for this approximation to be valid?

Answer: As N → ∞, the ratio K/N approaches a constant probability 'p'. Since the population is so large, removing items doesn't significantly change the probability of success for the next trial. Thus, "sampling without replacement" effectively becomes "sampling with replacement." The approximation is generally valid when the sample size n is less than 5% of the population N (n/N < 0.05).

2. If you sum two independent Poisson variables X ~ Poisson(λ1) and Y ~ Poisson(λ2), what is the distribution of Z = X + Y? Explain why.

Answer: Z follows a Poisson distribution with parameter λ = λ1 + λ2. This is because Poisson counts events in an interval. If you count events in two independent intervals (or types), the total count is just the sum of the averages. This is known as the "Reproductive Property" of the Poisson distribution.

3. In a Hypergeometric distribution, what happens to the variance if the sample size n is equal to the population size N? Explain the logic.

Answer: The variance becomes 0. If n = N, you are sampling the entire population. Therefore, you will always get exactly K successes (because you took everything). Since the outcome is certain (constant), there is no variability, so Variance = 0. This is mathematically shown by the finite population correction factor (N-n)/(N-1), which becomes 0 when n=N.

4. Can the mean of a Poisson distribution ever be less than its standard deviation? If so, when?

Answer: Yes. For Poisson, Mean = λ and Variance = λ. The Standard Deviation is √λ. We want to know if Mean < Std Dev, i.e., λ < √λ. This inequality holds true when 0 < λ < 1. So, if the average rate of events is very small (less than 1), the mean is smaller than the standard deviation.

5. Why is the Poisson distribution often derived as a limit of the Binomial distribution? What are the two simultaneous conditions required?

Answer: Poisson is the limit of Binomial when we have many trials but a very low probability of success. The conditions are: (1) n → ∞ (number of trials becomes very large) and (2) p → 0 (probability of success becomes very small), such that n × p remains constant (λ). This models "rare events" in a continuum.
